Title: I Didn't Come to Use AI. I Came to Build With It.

Opening — the declaration: You have been using AI for a while. For research. For writing. For thinking. It has been useful. But somewhere along the way you realised that using a tool and building with a tool are two completely different things. One makes you a consumer. The other makes you an architect. This is the moment you decided to cross that line.

Section 1 — Who you are without the labels: Do not list your titles. Tell the reader what you do and how you see the world. You make photographs. You design services. You have an MBA. You live in Helsinki. You think in systems and stories simultaneously. That combination — creative thinking plus systems thinking — is exactly what AI architecture needs. You just did not know it yet.

Section 2 — The gap you noticed: There is a difference between people who talk about AI and people who build with it. The builders are the ones shaping how AI actually works in the world — what tools get made, what problems get solved, whose problems get prioritised. You want to be in that room. Not watching from outside. But to get into that room, you need a language the machines understand. That language is Python.

Section 3 — The decision and the plan: Introduce the sprint. 16 weeks. One build per week. One publish per week. Two parallel tracks running simultaneously — Python literacy and AI architecture. You are not just learning to prompt AI. You are learning to write the code that connects to AI, sends instructions to it, and does something useful with what comes back. The goal — Claude Certified Architect and Claude Partner Network membership by July 2026. This is a building in public story. The reader will see the confusion and the breakthroughs in equal measure.

Section 4 — Week 1, told as a story: This is where your saved notes become material. Tell the reader:

What Python actually is — a programming language that lets you give precise instructions to a computer — and why it is the right language for working with AI
What a terminal is and what it felt like to open one for the first time as someone who has never written code
What an API key is and why you had to hide it in a secret file called .env
How you installed your first Python packages using a tool called pip
What hallucination means — Claude inventing hundreds of Finnish words that were never in your notes
The moment the Terminal Handshake script ran correctly and Claude returned a clean vocabulary list from your messy notes
What it felt like to realise you had just built something, not just used something
Be specific. Be honest about the confusion. Show the technical details but translate them into human language. You are the translator between the technical world and the creative world. That is your unique value in this space.

Section 5 — What you learned that surprised you: Pick one insight that genuinely caught you off guard. The hallucination story is a strong candidate — the moment you discovered that AI does not just process what you give it, it can fill gaps with invented content. And that preventing that is a skill called Prompt Engineering that you are now actively learning. That surprise is what makes this story honest.

Section 6 — A note to other creatives: Speak directly to the person reading this who is standing where you were standing before Week 1. The one who uses AI daily but has never thought of themselves as someone who could build with it. Tell them what you would have wanted to hear. Not that it is easy. That it is possible. And that Python literacy — learning to write code — is not a barrier reserved for computer science graduates. It is a skill you are building right now, one session at a time.

Closing — the invitation: Week 2 is coming. New model. New build. New post. Follow the journey not because the destination is guaranteed but because the process is honest. Every error, every breakthrough, every moment of confusion — it will all be here.
